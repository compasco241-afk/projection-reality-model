# Core Concepts of the Projection Reality Model

**Projection Reality Model — PRM**  
**Core conceptual vocabulary**

## Purpose of This Document

This document defines the initial conceptual vocabulary of the Projection Reality Model.

The goal is not to present a completed theory, but to create a clear working language for further development, discussion and critique.

PRM begins from the idea that reality can be approached as a structured field of relations. In this framework, what appears as an object, state, process or meaning depends on distinguishable relational organization.

## 1. Distinction

**Distinction** is the minimal condition for anything to be identifiable.

Something can appear as something only when it can be distinguished from something else.

In PRM, distinction is not treated merely as a mental act. It is a structural condition. Without distinction, there is no identifiable object, no state, no relation and no information.

A distinction may be understood as the first boundary of appearance.

### Working formulation

A distinction is the emergence or recognition of difference sufficient for something to become identifiable.

## 2. Relation

**Relation** is the structural connection between distinguishable elements.

A distinction alone is not yet a world. For reality to become structured, distinctions must stand in relation to one another.

Relations may express dependency, contrast, sequence, resistance, transformation, influence or organization.

In PRM, relation is more fundamental than isolated objecthood. An object is not treated as something completely separate from relations, but as a stable relational configuration.

### Working formulation

A relation is an organized connection between distinguishable elements that allows structure, interaction or meaning to appear.

## 3. Projection

**Projection** is the way a deeper relational structure appears as an observable state, object or process.

A projection is not an illusion. It is a real appearance of relational organization.

What is observed may not be the full relational structure itself, but its stabilized expression in a given context of observation or interaction.

For this reason, PRM uses the term projection to describe how relational organization becomes accessible as reality.

### Working formulation

A projection is the expressed form of a relational structure as it becomes observable, recognizable or operational.

## 4. Resistance

**Resistance** is the stability or inertia of a relational structure against change.

If every relation changed without constraint, no stable object, identity or process could appear. Resistance allows structures to persist long enough to be recognized.

Resistance does not mean immobility. It means that a structure has a certain degree of stability against transformation.

In PRM, resistance is one of the key concepts for interpreting persistence, identity, mass-like behavior and structural continuity.

### Working formulation

Resistance is the degree to which a relational structure preserves its organization against change.

## 5. Working Relation: WR

**WR** means **Working Relation**.

A WR is a structured relational unit that can preserve identity, interact with other structures and participate in change.

A WR does not have to be a physical object. It may be understood as a stable configuration of relations.

The term is intentionally provisional. It is used as a working concept to help build the model step by step without prematurely forcing it into an existing physical or metaphysical category.

### Working formulation

A WR is a stable relational configuration treated as a unit for analysis within PRM.

## 6. Change

**Change** is the transformation of relational structure.

In PRM, change is not simply motion from one place to another. It is a modification in the organization, state or projection of relations.

Change becomes meaningful only where there is distinction. Without distinction, there is no identifiable before and after.

### Working formulation

Change is a distinguishable transformation in a relational structure.

## 7. Stability

**Stability** is the persistence of relational organization through change.

A stable structure is not necessarily static. It may change internally while preserving enough organization to remain identifiable.

In PRM, stability is closely connected with resistance. Resistance describes the structure’s opposition to disruption, while stability describes the persistence that results from such resistance.

### Working formulation

Stability is the continued recognizability of a relational structure across transformation.

## 8. Information

**Information** is distinguishable change within a relational field.

In PRM, information is not treated only as data stored in a medium. It is treated more generally as a difference that can be recognized, transmitted, preserved or transformed.

A signal, a symbol, a state change or a pattern may all be understood as informational when they create or carry distinguishable relational difference.

### Working formulation

Information is a distinguishable relational difference capable of being recognized, transmitted or transformed.

## 9. Time

**Time** is interpreted in PRM as an ordering of possible or actual change.

This does not replace physical time as used in physics. Instead, PRM treats time conceptually as a relational condition that allows change to be ordered, compared and recognized.

Without distinguishable change, time cannot be meaningfully accessed.

### Working formulation

Time is the relational ordering of change.

## 10. Energy

**Energy** is interpreted in PRM as the capacity of a relational structure to undergo, resist or cause transformation.

This is not intended as a replacement for physical energy. It is a conceptual reinterpretation used inside PRM to describe the potential for relational change.

A structure with higher transformative potential may be described as having greater proto-energetic capacity within the model.

### Working formulation

Energy, in PRM terms, is the capacity of a relational structure to participate in transformation.

## 11. Mass

**Mass** is interpreted in PRM as a form of relational resistance.

This does not replace the physical definition of mass. It is a conceptual reading of mass-like behavior as the persistence and resistance of a relational structure against change.

In this sense, mass may be approached as stabilized relational resistance.

### Working formulation

Mass, in PRM terms, is the projection of stable relational resistance.

## 12. Reality

**Reality** is the structured field of distinguishable relational projections.

PRM does not treat reality as merely subjective. Nor does it reduce reality to isolated objects. Instead, reality is approached as the organized appearance of relational structures that can be distinguished, stabilized, transformed and recognized.

### Working formulation

Reality is the structured appearance of distinguishable relations.

## Conceptual Summary

PRM can be summarized through the following sequence:

1. Without distinction, nothing can be identified.
2. Without relation, distinctions do not form structure.
3. Without resistance, structure cannot persist.
4. Without change, no process or information can appear.
5. Without projection, relational structure does not become observable.
6. What we call reality is the organized projection of distinguishable relational structures.

## Status

These definitions are provisional.

They are intended as a starting vocabulary for further development of the Projection Reality Model. Each concept may be refined, extended or corrected as the model becomes more precise.
