# Limitations of the Projection Reality Model

**Projection Reality Model — PRM**  
**Current limitations, boundaries and open problems**

## Purpose

This document defines the current limitations of the Projection Reality Model.

The purpose of this document is to make clear what PRM does not yet claim, what remains unresolved and where further work is required.

PRM is presented as a conceptual research framework, not as a completed scientific theory.

## 1. PRM Is Not a Completed Theory of Physics

PRM does not currently replace established physics.

It does not replace:

- classical mechanics,
- relativity,
- quantum mechanics,
- thermodynamics,
- electromagnetism,
- information theory,
- communication theory.

PRM may use concepts such as time, energy, mass, information and signal, but it uses them at a conceptual level unless stated otherwise.

Any physical interpretation of PRM would require separate mathematical formalization, operational definitions and empirical testing.

## 2. Conceptual Language Is Not Physical Measurement

Some PRM concepts are currently conceptual rather than measurable.

Examples include:

- WR,
- protomass,
- protoenergy,
- relational resistance,
- projection error,
- relational stability.

These terms are useful for building the model, but they are not yet formal physical quantities.

They should not be treated as directly measurable entities unless future work defines clear methods of measurement.

## 3. Protomass and Protoenergy Are Working Concepts

The terms **protomass** and **protoenergy** are provisional.

They do not mean physical mass and physical energy in the strict scientific sense.

In PRM:

- protomass refers to stable relational resistance,
- protoenergy refers to capacity for relational transformation.

These terms are used to explore a conceptual relation between stability and change.

They require further clarification before they can be used in any formal scientific model.

## 4. Analogies Are Not Proofs

PRM uses analogies from physics, communication, information theory, cognition and artificial intelligence.

Examples include:

- radio communication,
- fiber-optic communication,
- signal transmission,
- mass as resistance,
- energy as capacity for transformation,
- time as ordering of change,
- reasoning as projection.

These analogies help explain the model, but they are not scientific proof.

An analogy may suggest a useful pattern, but it does not demonstrate that the pattern is physically true.

## 5. PRM Requires Formalization

PRM currently lacks a complete mathematical formalism.

Future work would need to define:

- precise terms,
- logical structure,
- formal relations,
- possible metrics,
- testable predictions,
- comparison with existing scientific models.

Without this, PRM remains a conceptual framework rather than a formal scientific theory.

## 6. PRM Does Not Claim That Established Science Is Wrong

PRM does not begin from the assumption that current science is false.

Instead, PRM asks whether different domains may be interpreted through a shared relational language.

The model is intended to complement inquiry, not to dismiss existing knowledge.

Where PRM conflicts with established evidence, PRM must be revised, limited or rejected in that area.

## 7. RRG Does Not Guarantee Truth

The **Reasoning Reviewer Gate** — **RRG** — is a proposed mechanism for improving reasoning quality.

It may help detect:

- unsupported claims,
- hidden assumptions,
- logical gaps,
- overconfidence,
- weak grounding,
- projection errors.

However, RRG does not guarantee that an answer is true.

It is not:

- a proof engine,
- a truth oracle,
- a substitute for expert review,
- a complete AI safety system,
- a guarantee of correctness.

RRG can make reasoning weaknesses more visible, but it cannot eliminate all errors.

## 8. Information and Communication Are Used Conceptually

PRM interprets information as distinguishable change and communication as propagation of distinguishable change.

This does not replace information theory, signal theory or telecommunications engineering.

Technical models remain necessary for real systems.

PRM provides a conceptual layer that may help compare different communication processes, but it does not replace their detailed mathematical descriptions.

## 9. Risk of Overgeneralization

Because PRM uses broad relational concepts, there is a risk of applying the model too widely.

A model that explains everything too easily may explain too little precisely.

Therefore, PRM must be developed carefully.

Each extension of the model should ask:

- What exactly is being explained?
- What is only being described by analogy?
- What would count as an error?
- What would force the model to change?
- What remains outside the model?

## 10. Need for Examples and Counterexamples

PRM requires both examples and counterexamples.

Examples help show where the model is useful.

Counterexamples help show where the model fails, becomes vague or needs correction.

A serious development of PRM must include cases where the model does not work well.

## 11. Need for Clear Separation of Levels

PRM currently operates across several levels:

- philosophical level,
- conceptual level,
- informational level,
- communication level,
- reasoning level,
- possible physical interpretation.

These levels must not be confused.

A statement that is meaningful at the conceptual level may not be valid as a physical claim.

A useful analogy in communication may not prove a claim about reality as a whole.

A reasoning model may not automatically become a model of physics.

## 12. Open Questions

Several important questions remain open:

1. Can WR be defined formally?
2. Can relational resistance be measured or modeled?
3. Can projection be described mathematically?
4. Can protomass and protoenergy be made precise?
5. Can PRM generate testable predictions?
6. Can RRG be implemented as a practical review workflow?
7. Can PRM be compared meaningfully with existing scientific frameworks?
8. Can the model avoid becoming too broad or too vague?

## 13. Current Status

PRM is currently an early-stage conceptual research project.

Its current value lies in:

- organizing ideas,
- creating a shared vocabulary,
- connecting distinction, relation, projection, resistance and change,
- exploring reasoning quality through RRG,
- building a structured foundation for future critique.

It should be treated as a developing model, not as a finished doctrine.

## 14. Development Principle

The project should follow a conservative development principle:

> Do not present as proven what is only conceptual.  
> Do not present as physical what is only relational.  
> Do not present as certain what is still exploratory.

This principle is essential for keeping PRM intellectually honest.

## Summary

The Projection Reality Model is an exploratory conceptual framework.

It may offer a useful relational language for thinking about reality, information, communication, change and reasoning.

However, PRM currently remains limited by:

- lack of mathematical formalization,
- lack of empirical validation,
- provisional terminology,
- reliance on conceptual analogies,
- need for clearer boundaries,
- need for examples and counterexamples.

These limitations do not invalidate the project.

They define the work that still has to be done.
